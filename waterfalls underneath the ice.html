waterfalls underneath the ice

Stop and write about “waterfalls underneath the ice” if you wish. Or you could go back to where the river begins.

I’m not ready to get out of bed. I’m not ready to throw back the covers and commit myself to being upright, stable, mobile, functional, a functioning member of society, a person who is actively doing actions with my musculoskeletal system and causing effects in the physical world around me. I’m not ready to imbibe that first cup of coffee, because that’s a point of no return—caffeine despite decades of tolerance still says OK! Time to go down the slide now! And kicks me off the platform of potential energy.

I’m not ready to see where that potential energy takes me, because why? I used to say something like “because I don’t want to be disappointed” but today it’s more like plain old because I’m tired.

I’m not ready to do anything about it though.

I’m not ready because I’m still hibernating. I have been for oh, about six or seven months; I’m supposed to be emerging from the cave now but I’m just—not ready.

What’s it going to be this time?

What is it that’s going to roust me out? Because it’s always something (until it isn’t, a quiet voice in the back of the cave says, and then it hits me—that’s it!)

That’s what it’s going to be this time—the lurking fear that one of these times is going to be the last time. And I’m like OK, fine, I’m getting up, I’m still here, spring, thaw, running water, crystalline waterfalls underneath the ice in the creek, tromping through the creek bed with my daughter, looking for wildlife, tree roots, ice sculptures, sunlight, anything glowing, fascinating, ordinary, magical, sublime.

I’m not ready to end this because the timer’s still going, but sometimes it feels like continuing is going to ruin something that should have just ended. But that’s OK. You can always throw stuff away later if you want to. Words are cheap. That’s one of the best lessons I ever learned about writing, and I learned it by typing on index cards.

I’m not ready to let go of typewriters and index cards but I’m also not ready to commit to using them again (why?) but I’ve also never found a digital substitute, because there isn’t one.

Same river twice—yeah, you know that whole thing? This is that. You can’t go back to the same little apartment and the same notched and scarred old table by the same window in the same little MKE neighborhood and write the same stuff, ever again. It never repeats exactly.

I mean when you think about it, why would you want it to? You already wrote all that stuff. %0aYou need to write something new if you’re going to emerge from the cave again and wake up and blink in the sun and start looking for honey.
