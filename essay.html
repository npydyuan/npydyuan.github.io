What is an essay?

For our purposes, think of an essay as a nonfiction piece of writing that contains a point of view. It is not a fictional story, and it is not a strictly factual report. It is an attempt to think through a question that may have more than one possible answer. Your voice is welcome in it. You can use “I” pronouns if you wish. We write essays not just to communicate a conclusion to others, but to make our own thinking more clear to ourselves. It is, as Penny Kittle says, “one writer’s views or wonderings, [...] written to make readers consider their own.”

The word “essay” comes from a french verb meaning “to try,” which we’re going to do if you’re up for it—try to expand the boundaries of what an essay can be, while striving for clarity and integrity at every step of the process. This powerful, flexible form of writing is often at its best when it juxtaposes the personal, the factual, and the imaginative.

To our existing skills and background knowledge we will add determining central ideas or themes of a text and analyzing their development, and assessing how point of view or purpose shapes the content and style of a text.
